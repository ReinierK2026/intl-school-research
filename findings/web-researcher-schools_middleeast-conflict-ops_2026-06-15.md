# Middle East Conflict: International School Operational Responses
**Research Brief — Reinier Kirsten | June 15, 2026**
*Prepared for: Director of Risk Management application, Taipei American School*

---

## Context

The Middle East has experienced two overlapping waves of conflict-driven school disruption since October 2023. The first wave — the Israel-Hamas war and subsequent Israel-Hezbollah escalation — drove closures and mass displacement primarily across Lebanon, Israel, and the Palestinian territories (2023–2024). The second, broader wave — Iran's direct retaliatory strikes on Gulf states and Lebanon — closed schools across the UAE, Qatar, and Lebanon again in early 2026. This brief documents what international schools specifically have done to maintain operational continuity across both waves.

---

## Wave 1: Israel-Hamas War and Lebanon Escalation (October 2023 – December 2024)

### Israel: Home Front Command Protocol

When the Hamas attacks of October 7, 2023 triggered full mobilisation, Israel's school system switched to **nationwide remote learning within 48 hours**. The transition was not ad hoc — it followed wartime rules drawn up in advance by the Home Front Command:

- **In-person teaching was only permitted where every student could physically reach a bomb shelter before an air raid siren completed its warning.** Schools that could not certify this remained fully online regardless of proximity to fighting.
- Reopening was **graduated by region**: central Israel and Jerusalem reopened first under these shelter-access conditions; southern communities near Gaza remained on distance learning far longer.
- Approximately **40% of schools in permitted areas resumed some in-person activity within the first weeks**, while the remainder stayed online.
- When Iran launched direct strikes on Israel in April 2024, schools nationwide closed again within hours, regardless of which phase of return they were in.
- Throughout, schools ran on a **hybrid model by necessity**: large numbers of teachers were called up for IDF reserve duty, and entire student communities had been displaced and traumatised.

**Key takeaway:** Israel demonstrated that a pre-defined, Home Front Command-authored decision tree — keyed specifically to physical shelter access time — is what allows schools to make consistent, defensible in/out decisions under fire rather than improvising each time.

---

### Lebanon: Closures, Shelter Conversions, and ACS Beirut's Response

The Israel-Hezbollah escalation reached its peak intensity in September–November 2024. By October 2024:

- Israeli strikes on Beirut triggered the **Lebanese Ministry of Education to mandate a two-day closure of all public and private schools** in the Beirut area.
- After that mandated window, **private international schools had full discretion** to choose between continuing online or attempting in-person — the Ministry withdrew centralised direction. Each school was effectively on its own.
- Nationally, approximately **900 public schools (73% of all public schools) were converted to emergency shelters** for displaced civilians, removing them from any teaching function for weeks.
- Over 60 school buildings were destroyed or severely damaged since the escalation began in September 2024.
- 77% of Lebanese public schools were closed at some point between September and November 2024, with UNICEF supporting a phased return of approximately 387,000 children from November 4, 2024.
- Schools in south Lebanon and in certain Beirut suburbs did not reopen in time for planned December 2, 2024 return dates.

**ACS Beirut: "A Source of Continuity and Calm During Conflict"**

The American Community School Beirut (ACS Beirut, founded 1905, ~700 students, American curriculum, IB authorised) responded to the October 2024 escalation with a now-documented institutional stance. On October 28, 2024 — during the height of the Hezbollah-Israel strikes on Beirut — ACS published a communication to its community titled **"ACS – a Source of Continuity and Calm During Conflict."**

This framing reveals a deliberate communications and operational strategy:

1. **Continuity as duty of care, not just service delivery.** ACS positioned school operations — whether online or (cautiously) in-person — as the institution's primary contribution to family wellbeing during crisis. The school was not just a place of learning; it was an anchor of normality.
2. **Institutional resilience as credibility.** ACS has survived the 1975–1990 Lebanese Civil War (including periods of single-digit enrollment and direct shelling damage), the 2006 Israel-Hezbollah war, the 2019 Thawra financial collapse, the August 2020 Beirut port explosion, and COVID. Each crisis was absorbed. This history is not incidental — it is part of ACS's risk posture and community trust.
3. **Private school autonomy used proactively.** While Lebanese public schools remained closed under ministry mandate, ACS exercised its discretion to maintain operations (online format) and maintain community connection. The school did not wait to be told what to do.

## Wave 2: Iran Retaliates — Gulf States and Lebanon (March–May 2026)

The conflict's second phase — Iran's direct strikes on Gulf states and Lebanon in retaliation for sustained US-Israeli bombing campaigns — produced the clearest and most relevant international school response data, because it hit some of the world's densest concentrations of international schools outside crisis zones.

### UAE: Overnight Remote Learning Activation (May 2026)

On the evening of Monday, May 4, 2026, the UAE activated its national early warning system following renewed Iranian missile activity. Within hours, the **UAE Ministry of Education directed all nurseries, public schools, and private international schools nationwide to shift to distance learning** until further notice.

What followed demonstrates what a prepared school system looks like under pressure:

- **Schools mobilised their teachers and administrative staff through the night.** Many worked past midnight, coordinating platform access, preparing lesson materials, and ensuring every student could log into learning platforms the next morning without disruption.
- The **Ministry's directive was total and immediate** — no regional variation, no school-by-school discretion. This is the opposite of Lebanon's approach and reflects the UAE's more centralised governance model.
- Schools' **top three priorities**, according to reporting from Khaleej Times and The National: (1) parent communications, (2) maintaining the existing coursework schedule unchanged, and (3) rapid technical readiness for remote access.
- The online period ran **May 4–8, with return to classrooms confirmed May 11** after the Ministry assessed the security situation.
- Exam schedules were tracked and adjusted, with Which School Advisor and Gulf News publishing live MOE updates on Term 3 dates.
- **NYU Abu Dhabi** closed its campus entirely during this period.
- Iran's IRGC had explicitly **threatened US universities in the Middle East**, prompting two American schools in Lebanon and Qatar to pre-emptively close.

**Qatar:** The Ministry of Education announced all schools would return from a specific Sunday following closures, though school leaders reported "caution" around the plan as "the situation has been evolving."

**Key takeaway:** The UAE model proves that a large, diverse, multi-operator international school ecosystem can execute a complete overnight pivot to distance learning — but only because the infrastructure (LMS, devices, parent communications systems, staff contact protocols) was already in place from COVID-era investment. Schools that had not maintained that infrastructure were exposed.

## Cross-Cutting Risk Management Strategies: What Schools Did

Drawing across both waves, the following strategies emerge as the defining operational responses:

### 1. Pre-built Remote Learning Infrastructure as Non-Negotiable Crisis Infrastructure
The schools that transitioned overnight in the UAE did so because COVID forced them to build the capability. Schools that had let that infrastructure decay struggled. **Remote learning readiness is no longer an academic continuity tool — it is a security continuity tool.**

### 2. Government Decision Trees vs. School Autonomy
Two distinct models operate side-by-side in the region:
- **UAE/Qatar model**: Centralised Ministry directs all schools simultaneously. Fast, consistent, removes school-by-school liability decisions.
- **Lebanon model**: Ministry sets a floor (mandatory closure window), then leaves private schools to decide. Schools with institutional resilience (ACS) used this autonomy well; weaker schools struggled.

International schools operating in Taiwan should note that both models depend on government communication reaching schools reliably. **If government communication fails, schools need their own decision criteria** — the Israeli Home Front Command model (shelter-access-based in/out rule) provides the clearest template.

### 3. Shelter-Access as Operational Gate (Israel Model)
Israel's protocol is the most directly exportable:
- Pre-test: can every student reach a certified shelter before the siren warning ends?
- If yes: in-person permitted under Home Front rules
- If no: online, no exceptions
- This is objective, legally defensible, and removes ambiguity during high-stress decisions.

**TAS Relevance:** Taiwan's air raid system, civil defence protocols, and TAS campus shelter capacity and access times are directly analogous variables. This framework should inform TAS's campus emergency protocol review.

### 4. Communications Framing: "Continuity and Calm"
ACS Beirut's phrase captures a strategy: **the school's role during conflict is to be an anchor of normalcy, not a source of additional anxiety.** This has operational implications — communications go out proactively, frequently, and with a reassuring-but-honest tone. The school does not wait for parents to ask.

### 5. Phased, Region-Differentiated Reopening
Neither Israel nor the UAE used a single all-or-nothing switch. Both reopened by zone, risk level, and readiness assessment. This means schools need a **pre-defined phased reopening playbook**, not just a closure trigger.

### 6. Staff Welfare and Reserve Duty Contingencies
Israel's experience — where significant numbers of teachers and administrators were simultaneously called up for military reserve duty — is a staff-continuity problem with no good last-minute solution. Schools that had cross-trained staff, maintained substitute teacher rosters, and documented lesson plans could absorb this. Schools that hadn't, couldn't. **A risk management framework that does not account for staff unavailability during national crises is incomplete.**

## Enrollment and Family Mobility Impacts

- Families at international schools in the UAE and Qatar began making **contingency relocation enquiries** to Singapore, Cyprus, and European school networks during the March–May 2026 escalation period. Bennett International (school placement consultancy) reported fielding urgent requests from families wanting to know how quickly children from Dubai and Abu Dhabi could be placed if they needed to evacuate suddenly.
- UAE international school **search volumes were up +90%** between March and June 2025 (the year prior), reflecting families stress-testing options before committing to the UAE market.
- Lebanon's international school community saw **significant expat family departure** during Sep-Oct 2024; precise enrollment data is not publicly available but Lebanon's diplomatic community in Beirut shrank substantially.
- Jordan's international school market (Amman) has historically absorbed displaced international families from Lebanon — Amman Academy and other top-tier schools report sustained waitlists, consistent with steady inbound demand from regional instability. However, the displacement caused by the 2024 escalation primarily moved Lebanese families within Lebanon or to Europe rather than to Jordan, as the speed of displacement was too fast for organised international school relocation.

## Applicability to TAS and Cross-Strait Risk

This research has direct relevance for a Taiwan context, which Reinier is assessing for the TAS Director of Risk Management role:

| Middle East Lesson | Taiwan / TAS Equivalent |
|---|---|
| UAE overnight remote learning pivot (MOE directive) | TAS must have remote learning infrastructure pre-maintained, not rebuilt in crisis |
| Israel shelter-access gate | TAS campus shelter capacity, distribution, and access times should be formally assessed |
| ACS "continuity and calm" framing | TAS parent communication templates for crisis scenarios should be pre-drafted |
| UAE school closures triggered by missile threats, not actual strikes | Even a PLA show-of-force exercise may trigger government-directed closures; TAS needs a protocol that activates before hostilities |
| Lebanon private school autonomy when Ministry withdraws direction | If Taiwan government direction is unclear or delayed, TAS needs its own documented decision criteria |
| Israel reserve duty staff depletion | Many TAS foreign-passport staff would likely depart in a serious cross-strait escalation; successor plans and cross-training matter |
| Bennett International family relocation enquiries | TAS enrollment is more stable (American passport Taiwanese descent students are unlikely to rapidly depart), but foreign-national families among staff and some student body may trigger attrition under sustained threat |
