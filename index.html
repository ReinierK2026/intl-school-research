Taiwan Relevance — Global Risks That Directly Impact Taiwan Schools

🇺🇸 US Travel Ban (39 countries, Jan 2026) → Chilling effect on American expat postings
The US travel ban expansion and immigration crackdown is reducing the flow of American families into Taiwan postings. TAS and Morrison Academy draw heavily from US-affiliated corporates (tech, government, military). A 10–15% contraction in American expat families would directly compress enrollment at Taiwan's most selective schools.
HIGH IMPACT
👩‍🏫 US Teacher Pipeline Freeze → Taiwan schools rely on American-trained staff
F/J visa complications, social media screening mandates, and the chilling effect on American educators considering international postings directly constrain Taiwan's international school staffing. US-curriculum schools (TAS, Morrison, Dominican) are most exposed — their accreditation requires qualified US-certified teachers.
HIGH IMPACT
🔥 60% Teacher Burnout Rate → Compound pressure on Taiwan's thin talent pool
Taiwan already faces difficulty attracting long-contract international staff due to geopolitical uncertainty. The global burnout epidemic means the pool of available, experienced teachers willing to accept Taiwan's risk profile is shrinking on two fronts simultaneously.
HIGH IMPACT
🏦 PE Consolidation (~40%) → Premium asset opportunity flagged
No major PE operator has entered the Taiwan market. If geopolitical risk stabilises (or is de-risked via TSMC diplomacy), premium assets like TAS-affiliate models could attract group interest. Conversely, independent schools face widening disadvantage vs. group operators in accreditation, staff recruitment, and ESG investment eligibility.
MEDIUM IMPACT
♻️ ESG Investor Requirements → Taiwan schools less ESG-mature than Singapore/HK peers
70% of parents and investors now prioritise ESG. Taiwan international schools have no equivalent of Singapore's Eco-School pipeline or Nord Anglia's Social Impact Distinctions. As ESG transitions from differentiator to investibility baseline, Taiwan schools may face difficulty attracting international partnership or expansion funding.

Taiwan Relevance — APAC Risks & Opportunities for Taiwan Schools

📉 East Asia Stalling (China flat, HK softening) → Taiwan competes for same expat pool
As China's international school market stagnates and Hong Kong remains politically uncertain, mobile expat families that previously chose Beijing/Shanghai or Hong Kong may re-evaluate Taiwan as an alternative. Taiwan's stable regulatory environment and competitive fee positioning (vs. SG/HK/Tokyo) are genuine advantages — but geopolitical risk remains the key deterrent.
POTENTIAL OPPORTUNITY
🎓 APAC Absorbing US-Diverted Students (82% HE growth) → Positive for Taiwan TSMC ecosystem
As APAC higher-education institutions capture students diverted from US universities, the broader APAC ecosystem becomes more attractive for tech-sector families. Taiwan's TSMC and semiconductor supply chain is a magnet for high-value expat families who are now more likely to stay in Asia rather than route through the US. This supports stable enrollment at TAS and Morrison.
POSITIVE SIGNAL
🇨🇳 China TNE Expansion (800K→8M enrollments) → Long-term structural competition
Beijing's transnational education expansion represents a decade-long structural competitive threat. While direct impact on Taiwan's Western-curriculum schools is low in the near term, Taiwanese families considering mainland partnerships or Chinese nationals in Taiwan may increasingly look to China's growing Western-affiliated programmes.
MEDIUM TERM RISK
🏛️ Host-Country Nationalism (Singapore, Vietnam, Indonesia reforming) → Taiwan's SIR framework a comparative advantage
Singapore (Jun 2025), Vietnam, China, Indonesia, and Thailand are all tightening beneficial ownership and local-hire regulations for international schools. Taiwan's Schools for International Residents (SIR) framework has remained stable with no major 2025 changes, making it comparatively predictable for operators and investors.
RELATIVE ADVANTAGE
✈️ China Corporate Relocation → Some families may consider Taiwan as alternative posting
China's regulatory cap on new international schools (no new IS for compulsory years) means multinationals with growing China operations face a constrained international school supply for their families. Some firms — particularly in semiconductor, EV, and advanced manufacturing — may route assignees through Taiwan instead, generating incremental demand for Taipei schools.
